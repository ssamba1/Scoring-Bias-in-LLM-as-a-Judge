# ISEF Abstract — Scoring Bias in LLM-as-a-Judge

**Scoring Bias in LLM-as-a-Judge Models: A 22-Model Landscape with Base-Instruct Comparison**

Sricharan Samba, South Forsyth High School

Large language models (LLMs) are increasingly used as automated judges to evaluate other AI systems, yet they exhibit systematic scoring biases that undermine trust in these evaluations. Prior work documented over 35 bias types but left a fundamental question unanswered: does scoring bias originate from pre-training or from instruction tuning? This study investigates that question by comparing base (pre-trained only) and instruct (fine-tuned) variants across 9 model families spanning diverse architectures and scales. Using a perturbation framework with three scoring bias probes — rubric order (scale reversal), score ID (label format), and reference answer (exemplar priming) — we collected 24,300 judgments from base-instruct pairs and 29,700 additional judgments from 22 instruct-tuned models. Across the 9 families, format-related bias (rubric order, score ID) decreased after instruction tuning in 7 of 9 families, with mean reductions of −0.42 and −0.72 respectively, showing that instruction tuning improves format robustness. However, content-related bias (reference answer susceptibility) increased in larger RLHF-trained models above 3 billion parameters, with the largest increase of +1.58 observed in Llama-3.1-8B. The 22-model landscape reveals Score ID bias has the largest average effect (Δ = 0.68) and widest model-level variation (range 0.00–1.80). Alternative explanations including global scoring shifts, single-family dominance, and parser artifacts were examined and ruled out. These findings demonstrate that scoring bias is modulated by instruction tuning rather than inherent to pre-training, meaning mitigation strategies should target the alignment stage and must address format robustness and content sensitivity as separate channels. All code, data, and reproduction instructions are publicly available.
