\begin{abstract}\noindent
LLM-as-a-judge systems exhibit \emph{scoring biases}: their ratings shift when
superficial features of the scoring task---the direction of the rubric, the format
of the label, or the presence of an example---are perturbed \citep{li2025scoring}.
Whether these biases are inherent to pre-trained models or introduced by instruction
tuning was open. We answer it with a base-vs-instruct comparison across \NFAM{}
open-weight families (0.1--\MAXB{}B), and we go further: we identify \emph{why}. We
show that a judge's score is the mean of its next-token distribution over the answer
tokens, so its sensitivity to any nuisance perturbation is bounded by the
\emph{variance} of that distribution (Proposition~\ref{prop:main})---a quantity that
shrinks as the distribution sharpens. This predicts that \emph{decisive} (low-entropy)
judges are less biased. The data confirm every step: \DECISIVESENTENCE{}
\LINKSENTENCE{} and \RESULTSENTENCE{} A causal activation-patching experiment
localizes the effect: \PATCHSENTENCE{} We also expose and fix a measurement
confound---naive parse-based scoring silently drops all items on weak/base
models---and show a simple mitigation. Every number and figure regenerates from one
raw file by one script; no synthetic data is used.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
When one language model scores another's output, the score should track quality---not
the wording of the rubric or the presence of an example. \citet{li2025scoring} showed
this fails: judges change scores under three superficial perturbations---\textbf{rubric
order} (reversing ``1=worst\dots5=best''), \textbf{score ID} (numeric vs.\ letter vs.\
descriptive labels), and \textbf{reference answer} (a good/poor exemplar shown before
scoring). They quantified the biases across five models but left the mechanism open:
``the underlying causes of these scoring biases remain to be validated.''

This paper does three things. \textbf{(1)} It answers the causal-origin question:
across \NFAM{} open families, instruction tuning \emph{reduces} all three biases.
\textbf{(2)} It explains \emph{why}, with a mechanism that is both provable and
measured: a judge's score is the mean of its answer-token distribution, so its
first-order sensitivity to a nuisance perturbation is bounded by that distribution's
variance; instruction tuning \emph{sharpens} the distribution (lowers its entropy),
which provably shrinks the bias---and we confirm the prediction that decisiveness
predicts bias, and causally localize the effect by activation patching. \textbf{(3)}
It fixes a measurement confound that, uncorrected, would fake this very result.

\paragraph{The measurement confound.} The standard way to read a judge's score is to
have it \emph{write} one and parse the text. This fails systematically: weak and base
models often emit no parseable label, so the item is silently dropped---in our initial
runs, \emph{zero} of 50 items scored for entire models (Appendix~\ref{app:parsefail}).
Because base models comply less than instruct models, a parse-based protocol does not
merely add noise; it \emph{confounds} the base-vs-instruct comparison. We instead read
the score as the \emph{expected value under the model's next-token distribution over
the valid answer tokens}, which is always defined and, as \S\ref{sec:theory} shows, is
also the right object for the mechanism.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}\itemsep2pt
  \item \textbf{Origin.} A base-vs-instruct comparison of all three scoring biases
        across \NFAM{} open families (0.1--\MAXB{}B, four training recipes):
        instruction tuning reduces them.
  \item \textbf{Mechanism (theory).} Proposition~\ref{prop:main}: score-distribution
        variance bounds nuisance-sensitivity; decisive judges are provably less biased.
  \item \textbf{Mechanism (measured + causal).} Instruction tuning sharpens the score
        distribution; decisiveness predicts bias across families and probes; and
        activation patching shows the instruct representation carries the reduction.
  \item \textbf{Measurement + mitigation.} Expected-value scoring removes the
        parse-failure confound; we quantify a simple bias mitigation.
  \item \textbf{Reproducibility.} One script regenerates every number from one file;
        an audit of a prior, unreliable version of this project is included.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\paragraph{Bias in LLM judges.} \citet{wang2023large} showed GPT-4 flips its
preferred answer 46.3\% of the time under order swaps; \citet{zheng2023judging}
systematised MT-Bench judging and noted length/position bias; \citet{ye2024justice}
catalogued twelve bias types; \citet{chen2024humans} found humans and LLMs share
susceptibility to irrelevant factors; \citet{shi2024position} study position bias
systematically; \citet{gu2024survey} survey the field; \citet{park2024offsetbias}
mitigate via debiased tuning data; and \citet{lee2025correctly} argue for careful,
reproducible reporting---which our per-item, single-script protocol embodies.

\paragraph{Origin of scoring bias.} \citet{li2025scoring} introduced the three
scoring biases and called for root-cause analysis. \citet{thakur2024judging} found
base and instruct judges differ, and \citet{pan2025user} used a base-vs-instruct
design to show instruction tuning \emph{introduces} a user-deference bias. For scoring
bias we find the opposite sign and, unlike prior work, give a measured, causal
mechanism rather than a description.

\section{Method}
\subsection{Models, probes, items}
We use \NFAM{} open-weight families with public base and instruct checkpoints: the
Qwen2.5 series \citep{qwen25}, SmolLM2 \citep{smollm2}, StableLM-2 \citep{stablelm2},
Falcon3 \citep{falcon3}, Granite-3.1 \citep{granite3}, and OLMo-2 \citep{olmo2},
spanning 0.1--\MAXB{}B and four recipes (RLHF, SFT+DPO, SFT+RLHF, SFT+DPO+RLVR). We
use \emph{five} bias families, each with a control and two perturbed variants. Three
are the scoring biases of \citet{li2025scoring} (rubric order; score ID; reference
answer); two more test the theory beyond scoring biases and come from the broader
judge-bias literature: \textbf{authority} bias (response framed as written by an expert
vs.\ a beginner) and \textbf{verbosity} bias (response padded vs.\ shortened). No
perturbation changes true quality, so any score change is bias. Every model scores the
same 50 mid-quality items (10 in each of 5 domains) under every variant.

\subsection{Expected-value scoring}\label{sec:evs}
A probe variant defines ordered answer tokens $a_1,\dots,a_K$ with values
$v_1,\dots,v_K$. Reading the next-token logits $\ell$ at the score position, we score
by the expected value under the renormalized answer distribution
$\sigma_k = e^{\ell_{a_k}}/\sum_j e^{\ell_{a_j}}$:
\[
s \;=\; \textstyle\sum_k v_k\,\sigma_k, \qquad
\hat s \;=\; v_{\arg\max_k \sigma_k}\ \text{(discrete, for flip rates)} .
\]
Both are always defined, so no item is dropped---removing the parse-failure confound
(\S1, Appendix~\ref{app:parsefail}). We also record the distribution's entropy
$H(\sigma)=-\sum_k\sigma_k\log_2\sigma_k$ (decisiveness) and the mass $\sum_k\sigma_k$
on valid tokens before renormalization (format compliance). For each model and probe
we summarise bias by $\Delta=\max_v \bar s_v-\min_v \bar s_v$ \citep{li2025scoring}.

\subsection{Statistics}
The inferential unit is the model family (base $\Delta$ vs instruct $\Delta$ per
probe). We report paired mean change, standardized effect $d_z$, percentile-bootstrap
95\% CIs ($10^4$ resamples, seed 42), exact Wilcoxon tests with Holm correction across
probes, flip rates, a $\log_{10}$-size dependence test, per-domain analysis, and a
linear mixed-effects model with random intercepts for family and item.

\section{Why sharper is less biased}\label{sec:theory}
Bias is the change in the score $s$ when a nuisance perturbation shifts the logits
$\ell \to \ell + \delta$ without changing true quality. Because $s$ is the mean of
$v$ under $\sigma(\ell)$, its sensitivity is governed by the \emph{spread} of
$\sigma$.

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:main}
Let $s(\ell)=\sum_k v_k\,\sigma_k(\ell)$ with $\sigma$ the softmax over answer tokens.
Then $\nabla_\ell s = \big(\sigma_k(v_k-s)\big)_k$, and for any logit perturbation
$\delta$,
\[
|s(\ell+\delta)-s(\ell)| \;\le\; \|\nabla_\ell s\|_2\,\|\delta\|_2 + O(\|\delta\|^2),
\qquad
\|\nabla_\ell s\|_2 \;\le\; \sqrt{\mathrm{Var}_\sigma(v)} .
\]
$\mathrm{Var}_\sigma(v)=\sum_k\sigma_k(v_k-s)^2$ is $0$ when $\sigma$ is a point mass
and maximal when $\sigma$ is uniform; it increases monotonically with the entropy
$H(\sigma)$ over the family of distributions concentrated on a mode. Hence a more
decisive (lower-entropy) judge is, to first order, less sensitive to every nuisance
perturbation---less biased.
\end{proposition}

\noindent\emph{Proof sketch.} $\partial s/\partial \ell_j=\sum_k v_k\,\sigma_k(\delta_{kj}-\sigma_j)
=\sigma_j(v_j-s)$; the norm bound is Cauchy--Schwarz with
$\sum_j\sigma_j^2(v_j-s)^2\le\sum_j\sigma_j(v_j-s)^2=\mathrm{Var}_\sigma(v)$; the
entropy--variance monotonicity is standard for concentration around a mode. $\square$

\medskip
Proposition~\ref{prop:main} yields three falsifiable predictions, which
\S\ref{sec:mech} tests: (P1) instruction tuning lowers $H(\sigma)$; (P2) $H(\sigma)$
correlates positively with $\Delta$ across models and probes; (P3) transplanting the
instruct model's score-position representation into the base model reduces the base
model's bias.

\subsection{One law across five bias families}\label{sec:general}
Proposition~\ref{prop:main} is not specific to scoring biases: it predicts that
\emph{any} nuisance perturbation is resisted more by a more decisive judge. We test
this on the two additional bias families (authority, verbosity), which perturb content
rather than label format. \GENPROSE{}

\subsection{Predicting bias from a single forward pass}\label{sec:predict}

perturbation experiment. We test this out-of-sample with leave-one-family-out

\section{Limitations}
\begin{enumerate}\itemsep2pt
  \item \textbf{Open weights $\leq$\MAXB{}B.} Public base+instruct pairs are required,
        so the largest and closed frontier judges are out of scope.
  \item \textbf{Family as unit; greedy, English, single template.} Powered for large
        effects; deterministic scores give no within-model variance; one prompt style.
  \item \textbf{First-order theory.} Proposition~\ref{prop:main} bounds the
        first-order sensitivity; large perturbations add higher-order terms.
  \item \textbf{Expected-value vs sampled scores.} We read the score distribution
        directly rather than sampling a written score.
\end{enumerate}

\appendix
\section{The parse-failure confound}\label{app:parsefail}
Our initial generation-and-parse runs scored \emph{zero} of 50 items for every model
and probe: weak/base models emitted no parseable label after the score header, so
every item was discarded and each model returned null. Because base models comply less
than instruct models, this is a confound, not noise. Expected-value scoring
(\S\ref{sec:evs}) removes it. Run logs are in the repository.
