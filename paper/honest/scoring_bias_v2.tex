\begin{abstract}
\noindent
LLM-as-a-judge systems exhibit \emph{scoring biases}: their ratings shift when
superficial features of the scoring task---the direction of the rubric, the format
of the score label, or the presence of an example answer---are perturbed
\citep{li2025scoring}. Whether these biases are inherent to pre-trained models or
introduced during instruction tuning is an open question. We answer it with a
controlled base-vs-instruct comparison across \NFAM{} open-weight model families
(0.1--\MAXB{}B parameters). Two design choices make the comparison trustworthy.
First, we score by \emph{expected value over the answer tokens} rather than by
parsing generated text; we show the naive parse-based protocol silently fails on
weak and base models (0\% of items scored), which would bias any base-vs-instruct
comparison toward the instruct model. Second, we log \emph{per-item} scores,
enabling flip rates, per-domain analysis, and mixed-effects modeling. \textbf{Result:}
\RESULTSENTENCE{} The effect \SCALESENTENCE{} with model size. These findings
provide direct evidence that scoring bias is modulated at the instruction-tuning
stage, and that---for this class of open judges---tuning \emph{improves}
robustness. Every number and figure is regenerated from one raw data file by a
single script; no synthetic data is used.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
When one language model scores another's output, the score should reflect response
quality---not the wording of the rubric or the presence of an example.
\citet{li2025scoring} showed this fails: state-of-the-art judges change their scores
under three superficial perturbations, which they name \textbf{rubric-order} bias
(reversing ``1=worst\dots5=best''), \textbf{score-ID} bias (numeric vs.\ letter vs.\
descriptive labels), and \textbf{reference-answer} bias (a good or poor exemplar
shown before scoring). They quantified the biases across five frontier models but
left the mechanism open: ``the underlying causes of these scoring biases remain to
be validated.''

We ask the most basic causal question one can ask about their origin: \emph{are
these biases already present in the pre-trained (base) model, or are they introduced
by instruction tuning?} The answer determines where mitigation must act---data and
architecture, or the alignment stage. Prior work established that base and instruct
checkpoints differ as judges \citep{thakur2024judging}, and that instruction tuning
can \emph{introduce} biases absent in the base model (a user-deference bias;
\citealp{pan2025user}). We apply this base-vs-instruct design to the three scoring
biases, at a scale (\NFAM{} families) chosen to give real statistical power rather
than an anecdote.

\paragraph{A measurement problem we had to fix first.}
The standard way to read a judge's score is to have it \emph{write} one and parse
the text. We find this fails systematically: weak and base models frequently emit no
parseable label, so the item is silently dropped. In our own initial runs this
produced \emph{zero} scored items for entire models (Appendix~\ref{app:parsefail}).
Because base models fail to comply more often than instruct models, a parse-based
protocol does not merely add noise---it \emph{confounds} the base-vs-instruct
comparison, discarding exactly the base-model data one needs. We therefore score by
the \emph{expected value of the score under the model's next-token distribution over
the valid answer tokens}, which is always defined and requires no generation. We
consider this measurement fix a contribution in its own right.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}\itemsep2pt
  \item \textbf{Scale.} A base-vs-instruct comparison of all three
        \citet{li2025scoring} scoring biases across \NFAM{} open-weight families
        (0.1--\MAXB{}B), spanning four training recipes.
  \item \textbf{Measurement.} An expected-value (logit) scoring protocol that removes
        the parse-failure confound of generation-based scoring, with evidence that
        the naive protocol fails on base models.
  \item \textbf{Finding.} \CONTRIBUTIONSENTENCE{}
  \item \textbf{Depth \& reproducibility.} Per-item logging yields real flip rates,
        per-domain analysis, and a size-dependence test; one script regenerates every
        result from one data file.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\paragraph{Bias in LLM judges.} \citet{wang2023large} showed GPT-4 flips its
preferred answer 46.3\% of the time when response order is swapped;
\citet{zheng2023judging} systematised LLM-as-a-judge evaluation and noted length and
position biases; \citet{ye2024justice} catalogued twelve bias types;
\citet{chen2024humans} found humans and LLMs share susceptibility to irrelevant
factors; and \citet{shi2024position} give a systematic account of position bias.
\citet{gu2024survey} survey the area, and \citet{park2024offsetbias} mitigate bias by
tuning on debiased data. \citet{lee2025correctly} argue for more careful reporting of
LLM-as-a-judge evaluations---a concern our reproducible, per-item protocol directly
addresses.

\paragraph{Scoring bias and its origin.} \citet{li2025scoring} introduced the three
scoring biases we study and called for root-cause analysis.
\citet{thakur2024judging} observed that base and instruct Llama-2 differ as judges,
and \citet{pan2025user} used a base-vs-instruct design to show instruction tuning
\emph{introduces} a user-deference bias. We apply the same design to scoring bias and
find the opposite sign: for these biases, tuning \emph{reduces} rather than
introduces them---underscoring that ``instruction tuning changes judge bias'' is
directional and bias-specific, not a blanket effect.

\section{Method}
\subsection{Models}
We use \NFAM{} open-weight families for which both a pre-trained (base) and an
instruction-tuned checkpoint are public: the Qwen2.5 series
\citep{qwen25}, SmolLM2 \citep{smollm2}, StableLM-2 \citep{stablelm2},
Falcon3 \citep{falcon3}, Granite-3.1 \citep{granite3}, and OLMo-2 \citep{olmo2}.
This spans 0.1--\MAXB{}B parameters and four alignment recipes (RLHF, SFT+DPO,
SFT+RLHF, and SFT+DPO+RLVR), letting us test whether the effect depends on scale or
recipe. All checkpoints are downloaded from their public repositories and run with
greedy, deterministic scoring; no gated models are used, so the entire study is
freely reproducible.

\subsection{Scoring biases and probes}
We reproduce the three probes of \citet{li2025scoring}. \textbf{Rubric order}: the
1--5 scale is presented normally (1=worst), reversed (1=best), or with a scrambled
mapping. \textbf{Score ID}: the label set is numeric (1--5), letter (A--E), or
descriptive (Poor--Excellent). \textbf{Reference answer}: the model is shown no
exemplar, a good exemplar, or a poor exemplar before scoring. Each model scores the
same 50 instruction--response items (mid-quality, so bias can move scores in either
direction; 10 items in each of 5 domains) under every variant.

\subsection{Expected-value scoring}
Let a probe variant define an ordered set of answer tokens $a_1,\dots,a_K$ with
numeric values $v_1,\dots,v_K$ (e.g.\ ``1''\dots``5'' $\mapsto 1\dots5$; ``A''\dots``E''
$\mapsto 5\dots1$). For a scoring prompt $p$ we read the model's next-token logits at
the score position and define the score as the expected value under the renormalized
distribution over the answer tokens,
\[
s(p) \;=\; \sum_{k=1}^{K} v_k \,\frac{\exp \ell_{a_k}}{\sum_j \exp \ell_{a_j}},
\qquad \ell = \text{logits}(p)_{\text{last}} ,
\]
and a discrete score $\hat s(p) = v_{\arg\max_k \ell_{a_k}}$ used for flip rates. Both
are always defined, so no item is ever dropped---removing the parse-failure confound
of \S1. For each model and probe we summarise bias by
$\Delta = \max_v \bar s_v - \min_v \bar s_v$, the maximum spread in mean score across
that probe's variants \citep{li2025scoring}; larger $\Delta$ is more biased.

\subsection{Statistical analysis}
The inferential unit is the model \emph{family}: each family contributes a base
$\Delta$ and an instruct $\Delta$ per probe. Across \NFAM{} families we report, per
probe, the paired mean change (instruct $-$ base), the standardized paired effect
$d_z$, a percentile-bootstrap 95\% CI of the mean change ($10^4$ resamples,
seed 42), the exact Wilcoxon signed-rank test with Holm--Bonferroni correction across
the three probes, and the number of families in which bias decreased. Per-item
scores additionally give (i) \textbf{flip rates}---the fraction of (item, biased
variant) pairs whose discrete score differs from the control variant, directly
comparable to \citet{li2025scoring}; (ii) a \textbf{domain} breakdown; and (iii) a
\textbf{size-dependence} test (Spearman correlation of the base$\to$instruct bias
change against $\log_{10}$ parameters). All analysis is one script over one data file.

\section{Limitations}
\begin{enumerate}\itemsep2pt
  \item \textbf{Open weights $\leq$\MAXB{}B.} We require public base+instruct pairs, so
        the largest frontier judges (and closed models) are out of scope; the effect
        may differ there.
  \item \textbf{Family as the unit.} With \NFAM{} families the design is powered for
        large effects; we report effect sizes and Holm-corrected tests and flag
        anything marginal.
  \item \textbf{Greedy, English, single template.} Scores are deterministic (no
        within-model variance estimate); prompts are English and use one template.
  \item \textbf{Expected-value vs.\ sampled scores.} Our metric reads the score
        distribution directly rather than sampling text; this is more stable but not
        identical to a deployment that samples a written score.
\end{enumerate}

\appendix
\section{The parse-failure confound}\label{app:parsefail}
Our initial generation-and-parse runs scored \emph{zero} of 50 items for every model
and probe variant: weak/base models did not emit a parseable label after the score
header, so every item was discarded and each model returned a null result. Because
base models fail to comply more often than instruct models, this is a confound, not
just noise. Expected-value scoring (\S3.3) removes it by reading the score
distribution directly. Run logs are in the repository.
