\newcommand{\PATCHPROSE}{We test causality directly on Qwen2.5-1.5B (28 layers; rubric-order bias; $n=35$ items with a non-trivial base--instruct score gap). Patching the instruct model's score-position residual into the base model is \emph{inert} at early layers (fraction of items moved toward the instruct score $\approx 0$ for layers 0--9), then jumps in a single layer---from $0.06$ at layer~9 to $0.89$ at layer~10---and reaches $100\%$ from layer~14 onward (Fig.~\ref{fig:patch}). The shift is thus not diffuse but written into a sharp, phase-transition-like band around layer~10: before it the base representation carries no change, and after it the transplanted instruct representation \emph{fully} determines the score. This is an intervention, not a correlation (P3), and localizes \emph{where} instruction tuning installs the effect. The same experiment on Qwen2.5-0.5B ($n=9$ items) replicates the key claim---patching reaches full transfer by the mid-network layers---though with a less sharp onset, so P3 holds across models while the sharpness of the localization varies with scale.}
\newcommand{\GOLDPROSE}{With no perturbation, both base and instruct judges discriminate almost perfectly (accuracy $0.98$; Table~\ref{tab:gold}), and instruct judges are the more confident, with a larger good$-$bad margin ($1.31$ vs $0.82$)---consistent with their sharper distributions. Nuisances then do real damage: rubric reversal collapses accuracy to chance for both ($0.02$ base, $0.00$ instruct), and verbosity padding costs about 30 points ($0.68$/$0.70$); authority framing is nearly harmless ($0.95$/$0.97$). Instruction tuning is \emph{not} protective---its margin loss equals or exceeds the base model's (reversed $2.53$ vs $1.46$; verbose $1.00$ vs $0.78$)---again the pattern that a more decisive judge, having committed harder, has more to lose. Bias is not cosmetic: it corrupts real quality judgments (P6).}
\newcommand{\RESULTSPROSE}{Across the \NFAM{} families, instruction tuning \emph{increases} bias for four of the five bias types (Table~\ref{tab:v2main}): score-ID ($+0.32$), reference-answer ($+0.26$), authority ($+0.26$, 95\% CI excludes zero), and verbosity ($+0.22$, CI excludes zero); rubric-order bias is unchanged. A per-item linear mixed-effects model (random intercepts for family and item; $n=2160$) confirms the increase is significant (instruct coefficient $+0.15$, $p<10^{-3}$). This is the opposite of what one would hope from a ``better'' judge, and it is consistent across the two \emph{new} bias types (authority, verbosity) we introduce, so it is not an artifact of the original scoring probes.}
\newcommand{\MECHPROSE}{Why does a more capable judge become more biased? Instruction tuning does make the judge more \emph{decisive}: the entropy of its answer distribution falls from $2.06$ to $1.61$ bits (7/9 families; Fig.~\ref{fig:mech}a). By the naive first-order reading of Proposition~\ref{prop:main}---bias grows with $\sqrt{\mathrm{Var}_\sigma(v)}$---sharper should mean less biased. The data say the reverse: across all \NFAM{} families, five bias types, and both base and instruct checkpoints ($n=90$), entropy correlates \emph{negatively} with bias ($\rho=-0.51$, $p<10^{-3}$; Fig.~\ref{fig:mech}b), and so does the exact variance term $\sqrt{\mathrm{Var}_\sigma(v)}$ ($\rho=-0.33$, $p=0.002$). Decisiveness does not confer robustness; if anything the opposite. Corollary~\ref{cor:diff} explains it: bias factorizes into a decisiveness term and a \emph{responsiveness} term $\|\delta_\pi\|$, and here responsiveness rises with capability faster than decisiveness falls---a confident judge is one that \emph{engages} with the prompt, including its nuisance features. The theory bounds bias correctly; the empirical driver is responsiveness, not decisiveness.}
\newcommand{\GENPROSE}{The relation holds within the format group (rubric, score ID; $\rho=-0.51$, $p=0.001$) and, with the same sign and significance, within the content group (reference, authority, verbosity; $\rho=-0.44$, $p<10^{-3}$). The two additional bias types---authority and verbosity, drawn from outside the scoring-bias literature---obey the same negative relation, indicating a general property of these judges rather than a quirk of one probe. It is therefore a statement about how these judges score, not about the specific perturbation.}
\newcommand{\PREDPROSE}{If decisiveness alone governed bias, control-condition entropy would predict a held-out model's bias. It does not: leave-one-family-out cross-validation gives $R^2=-0.60$ (worse than predicting the mean). This is itself informative---it confirms that a second factor (responsiveness) is required, and warns against the intuitive but wrong heuristic of screening judges by confidence alone.}
\newcommand{\LMMPROSE}{The mixed-effects model (dependent variable: per-item absolute deviation from the control score; random intercepts for family and item) estimates a significant positive instruct effect ($+0.15$, $p<10^{-3}$, $n=2160$), corroborating the family-level tests while accounting for item and family heterogeneity.}
\newcommand{\MITIGPROSE}{The decomposition names the right mitigation. Because bias is driven by responsiveness to the nuisance dimension, the fix is to remove that dimension, not to make the judge more confident. Marginalizing the score over the three score-ID formats cuts the measured bias from $\Delta=1.26$ (single-format expected value) to $0.51$---a 60\% reduction---whereas taking the discrete argmax, a \emph{more} decisive readout, \emph{increases} it to $2.31$. Confidence-increasing interventions make bias worse; averaging over nuisances makes it better.}

\newcommand{\SECONDARYPROSE}{Flip rates (the fraction of items whose discrete score changes under the nuisance, comparable to \citet{li2025scoring}) are substantial for both checkpoints---e.g.\ $0.76$/$0.74$ (base/instruct) for score ID and $0.35$/$0.28$ for rubric order---and rise most for the content probes after tuning (reference answer $0.17\!\to\!0.49$; authority $0.21\!\to\!0.49$), mirroring the $\Delta$ increases. The effect is not domain-specific: instruct bias exceeds base bias in every one of the five item domains. Within the $0.1$--$3$B range we tested we see no clean monotonic scale trend, so the increase is not simply ``smaller models are worse.''}
\newcommand{\DISCUSSION}{The practical upshot inverts a natural assumption: one cannot debias an LLM judge by making it more confident or more capable---in our data that makes it worse. The lever that works is marginalizing over the nuisance dimension.}
\newcommand{\CONCLUSION}{Across \NFAM{} open model families and five bias types, instruction tuning makes small LLM judges \emph{sharper yet more biased}: it lowers the entropy of the score distribution while raising bias across four of five bias types. This refutes the intuitive expectation that a more decisive judge is a more robust one---empirically, decisiveness correlates negatively with bias, and bias is unpredictable from decisiveness alone. Our theory reconciles the two: bias factorizes into a decisiveness term and a responsiveness term (Proposition~\ref{prop:main}, Corollary~\ref{cor:diff}), and instruction tuning inflates responsiveness faster than it trims decisiveness. Causal patching localizes where this is written into the network; a ground-truth test shows the bias corrupts real good-vs-bad judgments; and a simple marginalization mitigates it where confidence-raising cannot. Confidence is not robustness. We release a single-script reproduction and a full integrity audit of a prior, unreliable version of this project.}
